Generalized Symmetries Journal Club

This is a journal club that seeks to learn and discuss modern advancements in the understandings of so-called generalized symmetries or higher-form symmetries, and their consequences in condensed matter systems. These are symmetries that act on extended objects, i.e. strings or membranes. While the literature on this topic originates in the high-energy community, it has recently (in the past few years) become apparent that they play an important role in condensed matter physics as well. Indeed, one might say that we are in the midst of a renaissance in our understanding of symmetries. As one example, by extending Landau's paradigm of symmetry breaking to include higher-form symmetries, many "beyond-Landau" phases (such as topological phases) may be classified by spontaneously broken higher-form symmetries. The goal of this club is to learn the formalism and applications, prior knowledge is not expected.

Useful Background Material

The generalized symmetries concept was laid out in the following paper in 2015:

  • Generalized global symmetries
    • Authors: Davide Gaiotto, Anton Kapustin, Nathan Seiberg, Brian Willett
    • Link: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/JHEP02(2015)172

A review of generalized symmetries in condensed matter has been written by McGreevy in 2022

  • Generalized Symmetries in Condensed Matter
    • Authors: John McGreevy
    • Link: https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/10.1146/annurev-conmatphys-040721-021029

The formalism for studying and talking about generalized symmetries is based in the language of (quantum) field theory. It features a healthy dose of differential geometry (exterior calculus of differential forms) and topology (homotopy and (co)homology). For those without background, it would be useful to learn some elementary differential geometry, in particular about differential forms. See, for example, the beginning of the book Gauge Fields, Knots And Gravity by Baez and Muniain. Additionally, some knowledge of elementary topology is useful, in particular homotopy, homology, and cohomology. See, for example, the introductions to each of the corresponding chapters in Algebraic Topology by Hatcher. For an overview of the interplay of differential forms and topology (Hodge theory), read the introduction of Differential Forms in Algebraic Topology by Bott and Tu.
